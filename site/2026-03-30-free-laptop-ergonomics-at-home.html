Free Laptop Ergonomics at Home

Published 2026-03-30

**Why Good Posture Helps**

Working on a laptop often means hunching over. This can lead to neck pain, back pain, and tired eyes. Simple adjustments can prevent these problems. You don't need expensive gear to start feeling better. Most solutions are already in your home.

**Start With Your Chair**

Your dining chair or a basic desk chair is fine to begin. Sit all the way back in the chair. Your feet should be flat on the floor. If they dangle, use a sturdy shoebox or a stack of thick books as a temporary footrest. For your lower back, roll up a towel or use a small cushion and place it in the curve of your spine for support.

**Raise Your Laptop Screen**

The top of your laptop screen should be roughly at eye level. This prevents you from bending your neck. Use sturdy items like stacks of hardcover books, old phone books, or clean shoeboxes to elevate your laptop. Make sure the stack is stable and won't wobble or tip. The goal is to bring the screen up to you, not for you to bend down to the screen.

**Get an External Keyboard & Mouse (Even Cheap Ones)**

This is the most crucial "new purchase" if you don't already have them. A basic wired keyboard and mouse combo can often be found for under $20. Once your laptop screen is raised, you can't type on the built-in keyboard comfortably. An external keyboard allows you to keep your hands at a natural, comfortable height. A mouse is also much better for your wrist than using the laptop's trackpad for long periods.

**Support Your Wrists**

When you type, your wrists should be flat and straight, not bent up or down. If your keyboard is high, a folded hand towel, a small cushion, or even an old sock filled with rice can make a simple wrist rest. Place it in front of your keyboard, supporting the palm of your hand or wrist gently, but don't rest your weight on it while typing.

**Use What You Have for Light**

Good lighting reduces eye strain. Position your workspace near a window for natural light if possible. Avoid having a bright window directly behind your screen or directly facing it (which can cause glare). If natural light isn't enough, use an existing desk lamp or a clip-on light from another room. Angle it to light your workspace, not to shine directly into your eyes or create glare on your screen.

**Test and Adjust**

Once you've made these changes, sit down and try working for a bit. Your neck should feel straight and relaxed. Your shoulders should be down, not hunched up. Your arms should be close to your body, with your elbows bent at roughly a 90-degree angle. Your wrists should be flat. Spend an hour working and notice how you feel. Make small adjustments to your "props" until it feels comfortable and natural. Even these free or very cheap changes can make a huge difference in your comfort and well-being.
